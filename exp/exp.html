Pyrosoma atlanticum Grazing in the Southern California Current Ecosystem

Sophia Wu1, Grace Cawley2, Nilusha Wanniappa2, Dr. Moira DÃ©cima2

1Peddie School, Hightstown NJ, 2Scripps Institution of Oceanography, San Diego CA

Abstract

The significant expansion and density increase of Pyrosoma atlanticum off the west coast of the United States and British Columbia coupled with resulting massive blooms may have lasting effects on the ecosystem dynamics of California Current Large Marine Ecosystem (CCLME) and West Coast human industries. While previous studies have determined the effects of P. atlanticum in the Indian Ocean and the Northern California Current on phytoplankton or have used stable isotope and fatty acid analysis to examine the general trophic level pyrosomes occupied in the CCMLE, no research approaches have used gut pigment analysis and stable isotope analysis in the Southern California Current to analyze phytoplankton consumption, which is what this research aims to do. Pyrosomes collected in oblique bongo tows from the CCE-P2107 cruise under the CCE-LTER Process cruises aboard the R/V Roger Revelle through July and August 2021 were halved, with chlorophyll a extracted with acetone in half for gut pigment, and half dried for stable isotope analysis. Length was found to vary significantly based on location, as offshore samples were significantly shorter than nearshore samples (Kruskal-Wallis, p <0.05). In addition to longitude and latitude, factors such as temperature, salinity, and sunlight may also impact phytoplankton consumption, as there was a significant difference in gut pigment normalized by wet weight between the closely located pair of nearshore samples. Diel vertical migration may also influence the length and gut pigment of pyrosomes collected in hauls, as longer pyrosomes with greater gut pigment normalized by wet weight are found in night tows compared to day tows. Observations regarding length and consumption will impact research working to convert gut pigment into grazing rate per day, which will be used to calculate the grazing impact of the SCC pyrosome population.
